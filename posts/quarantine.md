# Quarantine
##### 2020.09.15

There is 1 days left before I return to school physically. It has been 184 days.

## thoughts going in

When the announcement broke that school wasn't going to start up immediately after March break, I was looking forward to having time to work on what I wanted to. Robotics being cancelled was very disappointing, but at the time I thought an extended break made up for it. The first week was great. I started making an RPG and got 2000 lines of code done. The next week was decent with 1000 lines of code completed. Quickly my productivity took a nosedive and as the school return date kept getting extended, I started to realize what I was in for. 

A lot happened over the 6 month period, and I can't be bothered to put everything chronologically, so here is a big list of things I learned, thought, and did. 

## Exercise

I no longer remember why I wanted to exercise consistently, but I wanted to and worked on making it a habit. Early on, I only walked. 3-4 times a week I got on my steel toed, steel soled, construction site approved work boots and walked over to the ponds/forest just over 1km away from my house. I usually spent 2 hours just going around trying to find all the unmarked paths and where they went. I started to occasionally go on runs after not wanting to spend 2 hours to get a good workout in. A few months in I suggested to my geographically close friend that we workout together. We started doing body weight exercises together 3 times a week. The accountability gained by working out with someone made it easy to not miss a day, and stick to a consistent schedule. Towards the end, I picked up running more seriously, and signed up for a 5km race with my dad. I did not end up looking like an absolute chad, but I improved my strength and endurance, and maintained my weight through 6 months of general inactivity.

## Social

Seeing that I refuse to leave the snack table at large gatherings, and that I will go a whole semester in a class without talking, I thought I did not enjoy socializing. I was wrong. Often we don't appreciate something until we no longer have it, and socializing was one of those things. Going so long without talking to people outside my family made me realize how much I had come to love being able to talk to people, especially people that are better than me at something and have interesting ideas. After 11 days apart, my school lunch group and I made a habit of hanging out in a discord voice call for a few hours most days. Any time I went a few days without calling, I felt off. Before this I had always thought of myself as an introvert, and never realized I actually enjoy being around people sometimes.

A few times closer to the end of quarantine, I met up with people in real life. I was always conscious of how I had completely forgot how to stand or sit in front of someone while talking. After months of communication with just audio from my chair, it was shocking how unnatural it felt to physically stand in front of a person. 

## Passions

I don't like the word passion because of the intensity it implies. I like to program, it is fulfilling, but I won't say I'm passionate about it. Saying that makes me feel like a 40 year old substitute teacher trying introducing himself. It is also used too generally, there are things that I like to do and do a lot. Those aren't the same as the things I do that are actually fulfilling to do. The problem with finding what you actually like to do, is that you need opportunities to try as many things as possible. I had time to try many things and figured out 2 more things that are fulfilling on top of programming. First is walking, yep, just walking. I went to the ponds near my house over 40 times during quarantine. Doing nothing but walking alone for 2 hours is incredibly meditative. I even built onto [a spot](https://rsninja.dev/photos/view.html?id=3) by the shore that I would occasionally go sit at for a while. The other thing I discovered I love is photography. I took many photos as a kid with my ipod, but never continued in the past few years because I wasn't happy with how low quality the pictures were, and how little control I had. Since I had only bought 1 thing in the last year, and was working weekly, I decided to splurge on a DSLR camera. What I enjoy about photography is how I'm able to capture how I see the world, and have others see the same things. I always head out to a photo shoot with something in mind that I really like to be around or look at. I hope to continue to get opportunities to try doing new stuff.

## Motivation 

When I think of motivation, I think of motivation for hobbies or skills I'm working on on my own. Things like eating, working, and chores aren't as hard to find motivation for since there are clear consequences for not getting them done, such as dying from starvation, or my room becoming unlivable due to lack of cleanliness. Finding a drive for doing my own projects is much harder. The motivation must be internal, for the most part there is nothing external that will push me to accomplish what I plan. It is hard to predict what will happen years in the future given my actions, making it hard to see the benefits of what I am working on. I used to think that my motivation was stifled by time consuming routines like school and work. After having so much time free of any time sucking commitments I realized that the amount of time I have free has little to no effect on what I get done. 

The main thing I don't understand yet is how I always get a boost in motivation when my schedule changes drastically. I'm always able to get a bunch of work done on projects, and then I slow down until I reach some sort of baseline.

I have learned a bit about what helps me feel motivated. Here's a list.
* eating properly
* getting pressing tasks done quickly
* not sleeping in
* staying fit
* maintaining a clear idea of what I want to do
* reducing easy dopamine (more in depth post about this later)
* making a todo list the day before so I don't have to think about what I will focus on

But I found the biggest factor to be what I did first thing in the morning. If I got up and ate breakfast without listening to anything, watching any videos, or checking apps, It was much easier to work on projects. If I slept in then played clash of clans first thing in the morning, I would hardly do anything the whole day.

## Work 

After 3 years working as a martial arts instructor, I finally quit the job. The first day I worked there I disliked it. The last day I worked there I hated it. Teaching kids is definitely not for me. I recently started to work for a guy down the street. I spend 2 hours a day weeding and it is bliss compared to teaching. Being able to just do unskilled labor, or do a job where I can communicate with adults instead is so much more enjoyable. ⚠ The rest of this section will be me ranting, please skip to the next section. The biggest thing I disliked was the pressure to be perfect. The owner was very good at teaching, and was involved in classes quite a bit. Problem is that he always pressures everyone to teach as intensely as he does. He would routinely put 2 of the teachers against each other and have the kids vote on who was the loudest running a warm up or kata. If a kid in the group you are teaching looked away for a second, he would call you out "why is *kid's name* not paying attention?". Parents were encouraged to stay and watch the whole class, so you had the added pressure of making sure someone's precious child got enough attention, was learning enough, and never got hurt. Another anxiety inducing expectation is having to ensure a kid learns fast enough when they are almost incapable of doing karate, or are being as annoying as possible because their parents forced them to be there. When I went through the system, you were only expected to test for the next belt level when an instructor thought you were ready. The owner changed the system when minimum wage was increased. He developed a new system so he could have less staff on the floor every night. Now every kid tests at once, even if they know only half of what's "expected". They progress through the belts as fast as the kid who can perform everything flawlessly can. It felt wrong giving kids credit for something they absolutely do not deserve. All of that on top of a general anxiety about talking to people I don't know well made the work unbearable. If I had to go into work in the next few days, I would constantly be stressing over it. When I finally decided to quit, when talking to the owner, he made fun of me for being anxious going into work and I'm still pissed off about it. Now that I'm out I've been feeling bad much less. I still like martial arts though, and want to maybe join an MMA gym later on. 

## thoughts coming out (hopefully for good (and no not that kind of coming out))

I think that this time was very helpful, giving me a chance to think through many things, and change up things about my life. I heavily miss having the routine of going to school, and will enjoy seeing people around my age again. I'm very thankful that I was in a household with a stable income during this time, giving me the opportunity to reflect and improve, instead of worrying about surviving. I'm not confident that the low case numbers will stay stable, but I'm hoping.
